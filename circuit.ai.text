## 2. PCB Layout Review Checklist

### üèóÔ∏è Stackup & Grounding (The #1 Failure Point)
*   **Ground Continuity:** Is there a solid, unbroken ground plane directly under all signal layers?
    *   *Critical:* Never route high-speed signals over a split/gap in the ground plane.
*   **Layer Strategy:**
    *   *4-Layer Best Practice:* Signal / Ground / Power (or Ground) / Signal.
    *   *2-Layer Risk:* Minimize signal loops; use "ground pour" on both sides and stitch heavily with vias.
*   **Analog/Digital Separation:** Do not split ground planes unless absolutely necessary. Instead, partition the board layout (keep noisy switching regulators away from sensitive analog/RF) and use a single solid ground plane.

### üõ£Ô∏è Routing & Signal Integrity

Additional "Pro-Tips" for your Design Reviews
While the markdown above covers the "Review" phase, here are a few proactive design tips I found during my research that you can apply before you upload:

"Paper Doll" Check: Before ordering a PCB, print the layout at 1:1 scale on paper. Place your physical components on the paper. You will be amazed how often you find footprints that are wrong (e.g., a "wide" SOIC vs. a "narrow" SOIC) or connectors that overlap.

The "3W" Rule for Crosstalk: To minimize interference (crosstalk) between high-speed traces, keep the spacing between the center of the traces at least 3 times the width of the trace (3W).

Via-in-Pad: Avoid placing vias directly in component solder pads unless you are paying extra for "plugged and capped" vias. If you don't, the solder will wick down the hole during reflow, leaving the component with a dry joint (poor connection).

Test Point Strategy: Every power rail (3.3V, 5V, GND) and every critical communication bus (I2C, SPI) should have a dedicated test point or a bare header pin. Debugging software without an oscilloscope probe point is a nightmare.
